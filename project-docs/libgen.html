Library Genesis Redesign

REDESIGN

Why Library Genesis?

This project was for a user interface design class, the prompt being that I needed to redesign a website of choice. Simple enough. I had recently been to Library Genesis in order to get one of my textbooks and thought "Hey, this site kind of sucks!" So, I set out towards the redesign process.

The Process:

"So what's the problem?"

In its current state, Library Genesis exists to offer free educational materials for users to download, however it is burdened by a confusing and overwhelming interface that detracts from its full capabilities.

As a file sharing site, it has been taken down multiple times for copyright issues, which explains why the site has never had any styling. The goals are very clearly to offer downloadable material such as textbooks and scientific articles for students or the general public.

The audience is targeted towards college students because they are often the ones searching for textbooks to avoid paying thousands of dollars on them over the years. The availability of scientific articles appeals to college students as well because it is a free alternative to paying for textbooks.

The target audience is reached as the site is well known amongst college students for its free resources. Students often discover the site through referral or social media posts, which lines up with the college student demographic as 70% are shown to be on Snapchat or Instagram and social interaction in college increases significantly increasing the odds that someone will get a referral.

The key components are the search bar, download section, upload section, and topics. These are the parts of the website that users need to find the necessary materials. Search allows people to find their books easily. The download and upload section are clearly meant for file sharing. People can use the topics section to narrow down their research goals and enable them to find content faster.

Library Genesis boasts several strengths. These are its functionality, its wide range of options for finding what the user needs, and its search bar is clearly the most important part of the home page.

However, the site has its weaknesses. Search specifications and menu options clutter the home page and the search results page overwhelming the users with unnecessary text and information.

The site also has an extremely bland color palette only utilizing the standard HTML color options, while ignoring the option to style fonts and positioning through CSS.

These weaknesses could be flipped into strengths by providing some structure and condensation to both the menu and search specifications while keeping the wide variety of options to choose from.

The minimal design choices stemming from the lack of styling allow for people not to get distracted by bright colors or weird fonts. By adding a modern color palette and some iconography, this simple style could be improved upon.

Goals:

My immediate goals for the site are to clean up all of the excess information on the home page and in the search results while also providing a simple color palette/branding guide.

By organizing the excess information, the user will not have to sort through text to find what they need making navigation more frictionless. The menu will be simplified by determining what is necessary and what is not for the site via a card sort activity giving the user a few choices rather than the current ten choices that span across two menus.

The color palette and branding guide will help keep consistency on the site to build trust with the user, as sites that look like Library Genesis often attempt to download viruses with fake links.

Card Sort & Site Map

The card sort activity was designed to help organize the site layout and provide some user input into what the target audience would want from the site. Luckily, my target audience was college students split 50% male, 50% female ranging from ages 18-24 allowing me to accurately test my audience through family and classmates.

I also tested someone outside of the target audience to see how their response compared to the college students.

Due to the layout of Library Genesis, only certain aspects of the subjects category were chosen to avoid going over the card limit. The user criteria contained in the card sort were as follows: 

  • Algebra
  • Art
  • Economy
  • Download
  • Upload
  • Donate
  • Other
  • Scientific Articles
  • Fiction
  • Topics
  • About
  • Multiple Blank Cards To Fill In
  • Russian Translation
  • Last Added

The testing resulted in a surprisingly uniform result with 67% having made 3 categories. Reviewing over the participants submissions revealed that users wanted the site in categories based on subjects, Library Genesis’s cause, and file sharing.

The only real outlier from this trend was taken from someone outside the target audience who sorted the topics into 7 categories, which created a much more specific menu. However, that was not common so this example provides the average answers from the activity:

This gave me very clear feedback for what the site map should and should not contain. Aspects such as Russian Translation and Last Added were determined to be useless by the testers because not only would it not help them find what they are looking for giving me insight on what should be removed from the final site map. The categories were arranged in a way that made it very easy to find what the user is looking for whether it is algebra, uploading files, or donating. To the left, the full map can be viewed as based on the average responses to the card sort activity.

Wireframes, Typography and Color Styles

The home page was redesigned to greatly simplify and condense the amount of information, while still providing options to define the user’s search further. By having the logo centered above the search bar, the site is more reminiscent of a common search engine providing familiarity to the site. The menu was condensed into 3 options via the card sort clearing space for the search area. However in this wireframe, the newly found space was not utilized as the menu is very large compared to the search area in the center. The extra logo in the corner was unnecessary. The search bar was too thin as the current wireframe has it crushed between two larger pieces. These were accounted for in the final prototype.

The search results page was redesigned to have a more modern and blocky look, as the original site has very linear search results. This was intentionally to separate the different books, as if you were looking through a library.

The idea was further improved upon by showing the cover of the book in the search results, which could help users distinguish books from each other or find exactly what they need.

Mirror options were removed to make the choice simple for the user with one download button. Search specifications were included here in case the user still could not find what they needed.

The flaws in this page also came down to spacing. The top area was too crammed together and the search results did not span the width of the page. This left too little space on the top and too much space on the sides.

Another mistake in this wireframe was the rounded corners. As nearly every corner was rounded, it created a more chaotic environment than anticipated. By decreasing the radius of the corners or removing it entirely this problem could easily be solved.

The fonts chosen were Merriweather Bold for the header text and Open Sans Light for the body copy. The contrast between the serif headers and sans-serif copy helps the user distinguish hierarchy of text on the website. As the header font, Merriweather Bold catches the eye because of its boldness and the serifs hanging off of the letters. The copy font, Open Sans Light, provides a simple, well-kerned option that provides enough visibility to be readable while not being the main attraction due to its size and light font weight.

Changes From What Was Proposed

In between the time between the final wireframes and the final prototype, some changes were made to improve upon the wireframes and the colors. Both the homepage and search results page had some flaws, so the first change made was to fix those. 

On the homepage wireframe, the area containing the search bar, logo, and search specifications was too small, so it was expanded to cover more of the page while keeping some white space. The search bar was also expanded in height to balance the center more. The logo in the top left was removed because it was excessive and the menu was shrunk down to draw the attention of the user towards the search area and increase the white space. A footer was also added for DMCA, copyright, and donations.

The search results wireframe was changed to take advantage of the space more. The search result boxes were expanded closer to the edges of the page to allow the results to display more information and the logo next to the search bar was cut out due to how excessive it already was.

Lastly, the color palette was adjusted to avoid the bruise like coloration from before. The navy blue also matched the current logo of the website making it simple to keep consistent with the existing brand.

The reds stayed the same, however the grey was changed to get rid of its previous blue tint and the off-white was lightened to pure white to avoid having another grey value.

The blue and maroon worked as accents, while the bright red worked as the main color to keep the site lively. The contrast between the red and blue also provided eye-catching attention of the areas of interest.

Conclusion

After these design changes had been finalized and the text and images had been put in place, the final website prototype had two complete pages.

The finalized changes for the home page included a larger search bar, iconography to match the specifications, the removal of the logo in the top corner, and the color change. The search bar’s larger size makes it more balanced between the logo and the icons. The iconography for each specification is chosen to match the word it represents, making user’s choices for narrowing down their search easier and more aesthetically pleasing. The logo removal from the top corner makes the space less cluttered and less repetitive. The shift from purple to blue in the final design works to the benefit of being aesthetically more appealing due to the contrast and synergy of the colors. It also matches the pre-existing branding to keep users connected to the brand they knew in the past without being unrecognizable. Lastly, the kerning on the letters in the text logo also was expanded to prevent serif overlap.

The search results page had its boxes expanded to fit the page, its logos removed, and its search specifications section expanded. The boxes containing each book as a search result previously failed to utilize all of the space on the screen. As each box contained lots of information, it was important to make sure that it could all fit within the boundaries provided. The text logos on the page were entirely removed or replaced to clear up space, as the users already recognize that they are using Library Genesis. The replacement logo was an icon to simply provide a way back to the home screen without having to repeat the logo or waste space. To further utilize the space, the search specifications box was increased in size to create some distance from the search bar. This prevents the screen from feeling cluttered by opening up a gap in between sections. Lastly, the corners on each box were made much sharper by reducing the radius of the corners to only 5 pixels.

The changes made to Library Genesis redefined it as a modern search engine with a friendly and trustworthy feeling. By using slightly rounded corners, stylized text and colors, and sophisticated iconography, the site appears safer than the average bare bones HTML sites that attempt to have malicious intent. All changes were focused benefitting both the user through building trust and the site owners by pushing the donation section on every page with the footer. Overall, the site shifted from a search engine that seemed like it could install a virus onto the users computer to a site that is designed for students to have access to free educational materials in a safe and efficient way.

How am I learning from this?

The process behind this redesign taught me a lot, not only in regards to InVision, the program used to make the wireframes, but also towards the process of a redesign.

In following projects, I have utilized card sorts, wireframe critiques, style guides, and how to assess the main goals of a site. The process became something I worked my way through, rather than just reading it on paper. And pretty much everything I learned in this project has turned out to be crucial in my following projects.
